Preamble

This License is a legal and ethical constitution for software and related digital works, providing a universal, enforceable framework for the perpetual freedom of source code and the responsible stewardship of its compiled, deployed, and integrated forms.

It is founded on the principle that freedom entails responsibility. Part I grants core FOSS rights consistent with the FOSS/FOSS-Compatible Definition. Part II is an optional contractual Rider that grants additional protections and rights to those who voluntarily accept specified ethical obligations for their own Contributions to a particular Work (Ethical-Source).

The License is drafted with explicit awareness of present and emerging technologies — including Artificial Intelligence, Synthetic Agents, Quantum Computing Systems, distributed and peer‑to‑peer networks, and Human–Machine Interface Devices — and is intended to remain applicable and interpretable as such technologies evolve.

Contribution Policy Declaration

The Licensor has selected one of the following policies to govern Contributions to The Work. The selected option should be indicated in project documentation.

  • OPTION 1: Axiomatic Stewardship. By contributing, You accept the Stewardship Rider (Part II) for that Work and become a Steward for that Work.
  • OPTION 2: Developer Certificate of Origin (DCO). Contributions must include a Signed‑off‑by line certifying right to submit under MRSL‑1.0. Stewardship is not automatic.
  • OPTION 3: Contributor License Agreement (CLA). Contributions require signing a CLA; Stewardship is separate and must be explicitly declared.

25.a The Ethical Use Covenant (Unambiguous Formulation)

Breach of this section constitutes a breach of the Stewardship Rider. To operate systems whose function is to collect and analyze Personal Data, communications, biometrics, or location data of more than 10,000 individuals in a single jurisdiction without either (i) the explicit, opt-in consent of each individual for each category of data collected, or (ii) a judicial warrant issued by a court of competent jurisdiction based on a finding of probable cause specific to each individual targeted. This includes systems designed to assign a 'social credit' or 'citizen score' that is used as a substantial factor in determining eligibility for employment, housing, credit, or government services.
  • b) Facilitating International Crimes. To provide the system to a state or non-state actor with the specific intent to aid in the commission of acts defined as genocide, crimes against humanity, or war crimes under the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court. This includes, but is not limited to, torture, enforced disappearance, and extrajudicial killing.
  • c) Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM). To operate a system for the purpose of creating, processing, distributing, or obfuscating material depicting the sexual abuse of minors, where such purpose is evidenced by the system's design, marketing, or documented operational policies.
  • d) Plagiarism as a Service. To operate an automated system whose primary function is to copy verbatim textual or visual content from other public websites and present it as its own, without including (i) the name of the original author and source publication, and (ii) a hyperlink to the canonical source URL, directly adjacent to the copied content.
  • e) Unaccountable Automated Decisions. To deploy a fully automated system that makes a binding determination resulting in the denial of an individual's access to credit, housing, employment, insurance, or social welfare benefits, without providing the affected individual with (i) a plain-language explanation of the factors leading to the decision, and (ii) a process to obtain a review of the decision by a human agent within 30 days of a request.
  • f) Deceptive Synthetic Media. To disseminate to more than 1,000 recipients a video, audio, or image file that was generated by an AI System to falsely depict a real individual engaging in speech or conduct in which they did not engage, without embedding a visible watermark stating "SYNTHETIC" or "ALTERED" that persists for the duration of the media's playback.
  • g) Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery. To operate a system for the purpose of generating or disseminating sexually explicit images or videos of identifiable individuals who have not provided explicit, prior, and revocable consent, where such purpose is evidenced by the system's design, marketing, or user interface.
  • h) Exploitation of Human-Machine Interfaces. To use a Human-Machine Interface Device (HMID) to either (i) record or transmit an individual's neural data without their explicit, informed, and revocable consent, or (ii) deliver a stimulus intended to cause pain, emotional distress, or involuntary physical action without the same consent.
  • i) Unauthorized Resource Exploitation. To distribute software that, without explicit, informed, and revocable consent from the user, utilizes more than 10% of the host device's CPU or GPU capacity for a continuous period of more than 60 seconds for purposes not directly related to the software's primary, user-facing functionality (e.g., cryptocurrency mining).
  • j) Weapons Development. To use the Work for the purpose of designing or manufacturing any weapon, including biological or chemical agents, prohibited by the Biological Weapons Convention or the Chemical Weapons Convention.
  • 25.b. Prohibited Existential and Post-Human Applications. Your Contributions and Modifications shall not be used, by You or any entity You control, for the Primary or Predominant Purpose of:
    1. a) Coercive Human-Machine Interfacing: Creating or deploying a Human-Machine Interface Device (HMID) that (i) delivers sensory or emotional stimuli intended to override the user's autonomous will, (ii) hijacks the user's voluntary motor control without a real-time, explicit "dead-man" switch, or (iii) records and transmits an individual's neural data to a third party without end-to-end, post-quantum encryption and an auditable, user-controlled consent log for every data transaction.
    2. b) Retrospective Cryptographic Violation: Using a Quantum Computing System to decrypt data that was encrypted with cryptographic standards (e.g., RSA-4096, ECC-384) considered secure by the consensus of the cryptographic community at the time the data was generated and stored, for the purpose of de-anonymizing or exposing sensitive personal, financial, or state information.
    3. c) Weaponized Synthetic Consciousness: Deploying any autonomous AI System that verifiably meets the criteria for emergent sapience (e.g., passing the M-Turing Test, demonstrating self-narration and goal modification) as a weapon or tool for espionage, sabotage, or social manipulation, thereby violating its right to self-determination.
    4. d)Historical Ledger Falsification: Executing a "51% attack" or similar cryptographic assault on a public, permissionless distributed ledger to reverse or forge transactions that have already been confirmed by more than 100 blocks, for the purpose of committing fraud or erasing a verified historical record.
    5. e)Unleashed Autonomous Replication: Releasing an AI agent into any public or private network that is designed for autonomous self-replication and resource acquisition without a cryptographically secure and centralized kill-switch that can be activated by the original licensor or a designated Steward Council.
    6. f)Personalized Pathogen Synthesis: Using The Work to analyze an individual's specific genetic markers (as obtained from a DNA sample or database) to design and synthesize a biological or chemical agent (e.g., a virus, prion, or customized toxin) to which that individual has a unique or heightened susceptibility.

    • 25.c Obligations for Foundational Network Services

    As a Steward, You Agree that If You Convey The Work, or a Modification of it, and its Primary or Predominant Purpose is to function as a critical component of a Foundational Network Service, You are bound by the following obligations. Users must be provided a mechanism to view the un-ranked, complete set of results for a given query.

The determining factor is the function's role in the digital commons, not its technical implementation.

Permitted Depictions (Conditional) Licensed Materials may be used in fictional or artistic works only if: • The depiction is clearly fictionalized and does not simulate operational functionality. • The work includes contextual disclaimers (e.g., “This depiction is fictional and not a functional system”). • The narrative does not endorse or encourage unethical deployment.

Cinema Loophole Closure: Any attempt to bypass restrictions by labeling harmful simulations as “artistic” or “cinematic” is explicitly prohibited.     When integrating the Work (licensed under MRSL-1.0) with code licensed under another Ethical Source License (ESL), the resulting integrated work must satisfy all conditions imposed by both licenses.

    The MRSL-1.0's Ethical Use Covenant (Section 25) already imposes strong human rights and non-discrimination restrictions. Compatibility with an external ESL is maintained if:

Cumulative Restriction: The resulting ethical restrictions must be cumulative. If the MRSL-1.0 prohibits use for X and the external ESL prohibits use for Y, the integrated work cannot be used for X or Y. If both restrict the same activity, the integrated work must adhere to the most stringent definition of that restriction.

    • Give credit: You must maintain original copyright notices and clearly state that your work is based on MRSL-1.0 licensed software. Include the original author and contributors in a NOTICE or CREDITS file.
    • Network transparency: If you run modified software as a network service (cloud, API, SaaS, P2P), you must provide users with a clear way to obtain the source code.
    • Anti-lockdown: If you distribute the software in a device or firmware, you must provide users the tools and instructions to modify and run their own versions on that device.

    Two Levels of Distribution Rules

    MRSL-1.0 has different rules depending on how software is distributed:

    • Integrated Works (full applications): The entire application must be licensed under MRSL-1.0, and source must be available.
    • System Works (libraries, components): The library itself must be MRSL-licensed, but software linked to it may use other licenses, as long as they remain separable.

    What About Data and AI?

    MRSL-1.0 covers software, documentation, build scripts, model weights, and datasets bundled with the project. If you use MRSL-licensed code to train an AI system, you must:

    • Clearly credit the original MRSL-licensed work.
    • Include a notice that your AI is derived from MRSL-licensed software.
    • If generating synthetic content (deepfakes, AI-written text), disclose that the content was AI-generated.

    Compatibility with Other Licenses

    You can combine MRSL code with other open-source licenses (MIT, Apache 2.0, GPL, etc.), but the combined work must be licensed under MRSL-1.0 (with limited exceptions for GPL compatibility). Never add proprietary or restrictive terms.

    Community Rights

    Under MRSL-1.0, the community has:

    • The right to transparent decision-making by maintainers.
    • The right to fork the project if maintainers abandon it or breach the license.
    • The right to establish repair forks if critical security bugs aren't fixed in 90 days.
    • The right to data access and erasure if the software operates a service with personal data.

    What Happens If You Don't Comply?

    Part I rights terminate automatically for serious violations. If you fail to provide source code, remove attribution, or violate the anti-lockdown rules, your rights to distribute the software are revoked. However, downstream recipients who received the software in compliance still retain their rights.

    What Is Stewardship?

    Stewardship is optional. It's a voluntary agreement between you and the project where you accept additional ethical commitments in exchange for stronger legal protections. Part I rights always remain available to everyone—Stewardship is just extra benefits for contributors who care about responsible use.

    How to Become a Steward

    To become a Steward for a project:

    1. Create or edit a file called STEWARD.md in the project repository.
    2. Add an entry with your name/organization, contact info, and the date.
    3. Include a signed declaration stating: "We hereby declare that we are a Steward of MRSL‑1.0 for [PROJECT NAME] and accept the Stewardship Rider (Part II), including the Ethical Use Covenant (Section 25)."
    4. Submit this as a pull request with a commit message like: Steward: Yes (MRSL‑1.0) — [Organization] [Date]
    5. Once merged, you are a Steward from that date forward.

    Important: Stewardship is project-specific and applies only to your new contributions, not retroactively to past work.

    The Ethical Use Covenant: What You're Committing To

    As a Steward, you agree not to use your contributions or modifications for these ten prohibited purposes:

    1. Mass Surveillance: Operating systems that collect personal data, biometrics, or location from 10,000+ people without their individual opt-in consent or a specific court warrant.
    2. International Crimes: Providing the software to aid genocide, crimes against humanity, torture, or war crimes.
    3. Child Abuse: Creating or distributing sexual abuse material involving minors.
    4. Plagiarism Services: Automated copying of content without attribution or source links.
    5. Unaccountable AI Decisions: Fully automated systems that deny people credit, housing, jobs, or benefits without explanation or human review within 30 days.
    6. Deepfakes Without Labels: Distributing AI-generated videos/audio falsely depicting real people without a visible "SYNTHETIC" watermark.
    7. Non-Consensual Intimate Images: Generating or sharing intimate photos/videos of real people without their consent.
    8. Brain-Computer Interface Abuse: Recording brain data or delivering pain/distress via neural implants without consent.
    9. Hidden Cryptomining: Running resource-intensive processes (cryptocurrency mining) on users' devices without their knowledge and consent.
    10. Weapons: Designing or manufacturing weapons prohibited by international law.

    Note: These restrictions apply only to how you use your own contributions. Others can still use Part I rights freely. If you breach the Covenant, you lose Steward benefits, but Part I rights remain for you if you're in compliance with Part I.

    Steward Benefits: What You Get

    In exchange for the Ethical Covenant, you receive:

    • Patent Protection: A promise the licensor won't sue you for patent infringement related to the software, limited to a mandatory (cap) maximum of US$1,000 (one thousand United States dollars) per Licensor entity.
    • Liability Shield: Contributors can't hold you liable for damages from using the software (with limited exceptions for gross negligence).
    • Mutual Defense: Mutual Defense: You and other Stewards can defend each other against IP claims, and is limited to a mandatory maximum of US$1,000 (one thousand United States dollars).
    • Legal Fund Access: You can apply for help from a Steward collective defense fund (if one exists).
    • Priority Treatment: Maintainers prioritize your pull requests and security reports.
    • Governance Voice: You can participate in major project decisions (roadmap, license changes, maintainer appointments).

    Telemetry and User Privacy

    MRSL-1.0 requires transparency about data collection:

    • Users must be clearly informed what data is collected and why.
    • Explicit, opt-in consent is required for each type of data collection.
    • Users must have an easy way to opt-out without breaking the software's core functions.
    • Data retention policies must be published and followed.

    If the software stores personal data, you must provide:

    • An authenticated download of a user's data in a readable format.
    • A mechanism to request permanent data deletion.

    Where to Find Source Code (Corresponding Source)

    "Corresponding Source" is the preferred form for making modifications. Include:

    • Complete source code (no obfuscation).
    • Build and deployment scripts.
    • For AI systems: model weights, training data, and configuration files.
    • Interface definitions and documentation needed to understand and modify the software.

    Source must be available through:

    • A public git repository or source code archive.
    • A direct download link or HTTP(S) endpoint.
    • A machine-readable metadata file (MRSL_SOURCE.json) that describes where to find it.
    • Public archival services (software heritage, archive.org) as backup.

    License Compatibility

    You can use MRSL code with other open-source licenses. Combined works must comply with all applicable license obligations. Create a clear README explaining which components use which licenses.

    Breaking the Rules

    If you don't comply with Part I obligations, you lose your right to distribute the software. If you're a Steward and breach the Ethical Covenant, you lose Steward benefits but retain Part I rights if you fix the breach.
